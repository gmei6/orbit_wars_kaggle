# Lessons Learned

This document tracks key insights, architectural decisions, and mistakes made during the development of our Orbit Wars agent. Review this before implementing new strategies to avoid repeating past errors.

## Performance & Architecture

- **Trajectory Computation is Expensive:** O(T) predictive simulations are too slow for real-time turn limits. **Fix:** Precompute trajectories and cache them per-turn. This optimization reduced turn execution time from >1.5ms to ~0.60ms.
- **Environment Emulation:** Relying solely on Kaggle's remote environment slows down iteration. **Fix:** We vendored a local version of the Kaggle `orbit_wars` physics engine and created `arena.py` for rapid self-play benchmarking.
- **Rigid Body Rotation:** Inner planets rotate at a shared angular velocity, maintaining constant relative distances over time. We can precompute and cache their travel times to avoid per-turn dynamic intercept math for inner-inner pairs.

## Strategy & Gameplay

- **Piecemeal Attacks Fail:** Launching greedy, sequential multi-planet attacks leads to fleets arriving one by one, making them easy targets for defensive reserves. **Fix:** Implemented `v1` Synchronized Fleet Arrivals. Delaying launches from closer planets so fleets arrive simultaneously increased win-rate against the starter bot from 37% to 69%.
- **Producer Lite Threat:** Our `v1` logic currently loses to the `Producer Lite` opponent (0% win rate, -2395 average ships). Synchronized attacks alone are insufficient against its strategy.
- **The "Shiny Object" Problem (Zero ROI Awareness):** Targeting strictly by production value ignores travel distance and enemy garrison size. It leads to fleets chasing distant, heavily defended planets while ignoring cheap, close expansions, resulting in massive economic deficits. **Fix (Planned for v1_1):** Evaluate targets based on Return On Investment (ROI).
- **The "Frozen Capital" Problem (Over-defending):** Subtracting the entire incoming enemy fleet size from our available ships immediately ignores natural ship regeneration during the enemy's travel time. This paralyzes early expansion. **Fix (Planned for v1_1):** Defense reserves must subtract the planet's expected production before freezing ships.
- **Same-owner Fleet Stacking:** The engine groups fleets arriving on the same turn by owner before resolving combat. This means fleets arriving simultaneously combine their force rather than being defeated sequentially, confirming our Synchronized Arrivals logic works correctly at the engine level.
- **Determinism = store the baseline, perturb it:** Orbits, comet paths, and any in-flight fleet are fully deterministic — fleets are committed straight-line projectiles that cannot redirect, so the only unknown is each player's *next* launch. Store the forecastable baseline as a per-planet **event timeline** (production rate + sorted arrival events) and treat candidate launches as cheap perturbations rather than recomputing. The old `calculate_defense_needs` just summed incoming enemy ships, ignoring arrival order and ownership flips — the timeline (v2 Stage 1) fixes that.
- **Economy is the scoreboard:** the loss to `Producer Lite` is a macro/economy deficit, not a tactics problem. Optimize the production-integral (total ships generated over the remaining turns), not individual fights.
- **The `Producer Lite` Macro Deficit Persists:** Even after implementing perfect mathematical forecasting and an unfreezing doctrine that guarantees safe capital usage in v1_1, we still lose to `Producer Lite` (0% win-rate). This implies the flaw isn't tactical miscalculation, but fundamental strategic misalignment (e.g., target valuation weights or reachability thresholds). We must tune the model thresholds.
- **Timeline Amnesia (Double-Booking Targets):** When delaying fleet launches for synchronized attacks, those reserved ships are "invisible" to the timeline because they haven't launched yet. The agent evaluates the target again next turn, sees it is still neutral, and reserves *another* batch of ships, resulting in infinite double-booking loops. **Fix:** Future unlaunched `RESERVATIONS` must be injected directly into the predictive timeline before target valuation.
- **Cowardly Expansion (The Reachability Trap):** Automatically dropping ROI to 0.0 for any planet the enemy can theoretically reach faster causes total macro paralysis. It assumes the enemy has infinite ships and will flawlessly attack everything they are closer to. **Fix:** Do not surrender targets preemptively. Valuing risky targets prevents ceding the entire board and forces the enemy to actually spend ships to stop us.
- **Snipe feasibility is a reachability question, not just arithmetic:** A replay vs `Producer Lite` proposed sniping planet 13 right after the enemy captured it (15 ships → neutral-7 flips at step 13 leaving 8; +5/turn → 13 at step 14, so 14 ships retakes it). The arithmetic was exact, but planet 13 was ~40u from our only base on the opposite corner — earliest intercept step 26, twelve turns too late. **Fix:** Gate any snipe valuation on the reachability race (can a fleet land inside the garrison trough window?) before scoring it. The math being right means nothing if the geometry is impossible.
- **Raw ROI strands the agent (travel time is not optional):** In the same replay we peaked at 2 planets while `Producer Lite` grew 1→19 and won (reward −1). Cause: `value()` ranks targets by raw ROI ignoring travel time, and garrison-release hoards ships, so the agent expands late and toward distant targets. **Fix (queued):** score ROI *per turn* (penalize distance) and release idle capital sooner. Expansion macro beats tactical correctness.
- **Reasons a planet sends ships (design frame):** capture, defend, deny/spoil, evacuate, consolidate, stage, comet-intercept, bait, pin. Two non-reasons ruled out by mechanics — no mid-flight fleet interception (combat resolves only at planets) and no scouting (full-information game). Model these as competing utility terms, not decision-tree branches (see decision D-014).
- **`value()` already had a travel-time term — it was just wrong.** The tracker said `value()` "ignores travel time," but the code *did* divide by a time term; the term was raw Euclidean distance (not turns), off a stale `initial_planets` position, and speed-blind (fleet speed scales with size). **Lesson:** re-read the code before trusting a symptom-based diagnosis — the fix was "correct the term," not "add a missing one." **Fix that worked (D-016):** reuse the reachability race's `t_me` (earliest credible-capture turn, already computed for the hold multiplier) as the time term, and return 0 when `t_me` is None. Scoring ROI per `t_me` made `v2_1` beat the frozen `v2_macro` 57% (200 games, +875 avg ships).
- **A valuation fix is not a macro win.** After the travel-time fix, `v2_1` still loses 0% to `Producer Lite` (margin ~−4200). Better target *ranking* does nothing if idle capital never launches — the binding constraint is now ship-hoarding (garrison release), not valuation. Fix one macro lever at a time and re-measure, or you won't know which one moved the needle.
- **Garrison release was a red herring — the launch gate is the real constraint (D-020).** The tracker assumed ship-hoarding lived in the `available[]` / `min_garrison` block. A `GARRISON_DEBUG` probe over one `Producer Lite` game disproved it: early-game capital is *already released* (`released ≈ owned`, `will_lose=0`), yet `sent=0` most turns because `strategy.decide` step 4 only launches when it can **fully fund a capture in one synchronized wave**. A lone base drip-feeds ~29-ship lumps every ~6 turns and expansion stalls at 3 planets (~step 15). **Lesson:** "released capital" ≠ "deployed capital" — measure `sent`, not just `available`. The fix is to let a base spend already-free ships (partial commits / parallel cheap-neutral expansion), not to release more.
- **Shrinking the freeze lookahead (lever A) was a measured no-op.** `FREEZE_LOOKAHEAD` 30→12 moved `released` by exactly 0 ships with `will_lose=0` throughout — the mid-game freeze comes from threats landing *inside* 12 turns (the M2 trough: step 40 `released=32 / owned=205`), and the planet is lost anyway. **Lesson:** before tuning a horizon, confirm the freeze is driven by the *far* end of it; here it wasn't. Lever B (reserve only the first-threat survival force, release the surplus) is the right tool for that M2 trough, but it is secondary to the launch gate.


## Tooling

- **Evaluation Bias:** Standard 1v1 matches can be skewed by starting positions. **Fix:** Implemented antithetic seat swapping in `arena.py` and Wilson score intervals for robust confidence metrics during evaluations.
- **Mermaid Rendering in IDE:** Native markdown preview fails to render diagrams if node labels contain special characters (like `:` or spaces) without quotes. **Fix:** Always wrap node labels in double quotes (e.g., `Node["label: text"]`) when creating `graph TD` diagrams.
- **Doc drift after a refactor:** when `src/` became `v1/` + `v1_1/` and the strategy/targeting roles swapped, the per-module docs were updated but the index/README layer (`README.md`, `docs/architecture/index.md`, `v1_1/README.md`) was not — they still describe the old `src/` world. Conventions require updating the doc in the same change; the habit to build is checking the index/README layer, not just module docs.
- **Python Imports in Tests:** We constantly fail to run test scripts (like `pytest`) because of `ModuleNotFoundError`. **Fix:** Always prefix python execution commands with `PYTHONPATH=. ` (e.g., `PYTHONPATH=. pytest tests/...`) to ensure local modules resolve correctly.
- **Offline Visualizer:** The Kaggle web visualizer is a great debugging tool but requires uploading bots online. We created `scripts/visualize.py` to generate offline HTML replays via `kaggle_environments.render(mode='html')`. Player 0 spawns in the bottom-left (blue) and Player 1 in the top-right (orange).
- **Parsing Kaggle HTML replays offline:** The episode is embedded as `window.kaggle = {...}` near the top of the rendered HTML — brace-match from there and `json.loads` to extract it. `environment.steps[i][seat]` holds `observation.planets` (`[id, owner, x, y, radius, ships, production]`), `observation.fleets`, and `action` (the move that seat submitted at step i). Owner −1 = neutral, 0/1 = players. This verifies claims against the frozen engine math directly, without re-running a sim.
- **`spec_from_file_location` must be registered in `sys.modules`.** Loading an agent (e.g. `producer_lite`) via `importlib.util.spec_from_file_location(name, path)` + `module_from_spec` + `exec_module` crashes its `@dataclass` with `AttributeError: 'NoneType' object has no attribute '__dict__'` — the dataclass machinery looks up `sys.modules[cls.__module__]` and the module was never registered. **Fix:** `sys.modules[spec.name] = mod` *before* `exec_module`.
- **Pin BLAS/OMP threads for process-pool eval; don't cap workers on a hunch.** `arena.py` parallelises with `ProcessPoolExecutor`, so a torch/NumPy opponent spinning its own thread pool inside each worker oversubscribes the cores. Setting `OMP/OPENBLAS/MKL/VECLIB/NUMEXPR_NUM_THREADS=1` (via `setdefault`, before the heavy import) bought ~6% vs `Producer Lite`. The companion idea — capping workers to the M1 Max's 8 performance cores — was **measured slower**: once threads are pinned, throughput rises monotonically with workers (10 > 9 > 8 over 80 games), because a single-threaded process on an E-core still nets positive. **Lesson:** hardware heuristics (`cpu_count==10` → 8) are guesses that also don't generalize (M2/M3/M4 Max ≠ 10 cores); measure, and prefer an `ARENA_WORKERS` knob over a magic number.
- **Copying a package dir keeps absolute imports pointing home.** When `v2_macro/` was copied to `v2_1/`, the copies' `from v2_macro.x import …` lines still imported the *old* package, so edits to `v2_1` silently ran `v2_macro` (and `arena` had no `v2_1` resolver branch). **Fix:** after duplicating a package, switch internal imports to relative (`from .x import …`) and add the new dir to any runner's dispatch.
- **`check_okf` doesn't catch `resource:` drift — only `type:`.** OKF conformance checks that every `.md` has a non-empty `type:` field; it never verifies that `resource:` paths resolve. So when `v1_1/` was renamed through `v2_macro`→`v2_1` and deleted, all eight architecture docs' `resource: v1_1/*.py` dangled while still passing `check_okf`. **Fix:** after any package rename, grep `resource:` (and code-path mentions) and repoint to the active dir — don't trust conformance to flag it. **Corollary:** test filenames outlive the dir they pinned (`tests/test_v1_1_*.py` survived the `v1_1/` deletion), so keep them as historical pins rather than blanket-renaming `v1_1`→`v2_1`.
- **Verify-first earns its keep — it killed a knob before a 200-game run.** A gated debug probe echoing both behavior (`released`, `sent`, `will_lose`) and the live knob value (`look=<FREEZE_LOOKAHEAD>`) cost ~20 lines and one local game, and disproved the entire garrison-release hypothesis before any arena time was spent. An identical-to-the-byte re-run after the edit was the tell that the change was a no-op; the `look=` echo then proved the edit *was* live, ruling out stale code. **Lesson:** instrument the symptom (where do ships actually stall?) and echo the knob's live value, so "no effect" and "not running" are distinguishable in a single run. Harness: `scripts/verify_garrison.py`.
